Chapter I

Origins & Early History

From a Saxon river valley to the pages of the Domesday Book — the deep roots of a Devon market town that has been here for over a thousand years.

Nestled in the valley of the River Otter in East Devon, Ottery St Mary is one of England's oldest continuously settled communities. The name tells its own story: "Ottery" comes from the river itself, while "St Mary" reflects the dedication of its great collegiate church. The fuller Latin form, Oteri Sancte Marie, first appears in written records in 1242 — though the settlement is far older.

1688 · The Glorious Revolution

In 1688, William of Orange marched from Brixham towards London to claim the English throne from King James II — one of the most pivotal moments in British constitutional history. According to local records, he stopped and dined in Ottery St Mary along the way. For one brief day, this quiet market town found itself at the centre of the nation's story.

Chapter II

St Mary's Church

Bigger than you'd expect for a market town — and that's the point. Bishop Grandisson built it to rival Exeter Cathedral itself.

At the heart of Ottery St Mary stands a building so grand it has long been known as the "Cathedral of the East." St Mary's was rebuilt in 1342 by Bishop John de Grandisson of Exeter, who deliberately modelled it on Exeter Cathedral — twin towers, massive nave, the lot. It was established as a Collegiate Church, housing a college of priests, until Henry VIII dissolved it in 1545. Today it is a Grade I Listed Building.

"Lying large and low like a tired beast."
— Sir Nikolaus Pevsner, architectural historian

What to Look Out For

⏰

The Astronomical Clock

One of England's oldest working mechanical clocks. It follows Ptolemaic cosmology — Earth at the centre of the solar system — a genuinely medieval way of understanding the universe, frozen in brass.

🐦

The Weathercock

Over 500 years old and widely believed to be the oldest weathercock in Europe. Still perched on the tower, still doing its job.

🏛️

The Dorset Aisle

A breathtaking fan-vaulted aisle commissioned around 1520 by Cecily Bonville, Baroness Harington. Pendant bosses, elaborate stonework — the finest Perpendicular Gothic vaulting in the West Country.

🎭

The Misericords

Ten carved wooden misericords survive from the original 1350 construction. Five of them bear the personal arms of Bishop Grandisson himself — carved signatures in oak.

🎵

The Minstrel Gallery

A rare surviving feature that once housed musicians performing for the college of priests. It's still there, looking down over the nave.

Chapter III

Notable People

Bishops, Romantic poets, missionary saints and visiting monarchs — the remarkable figures who called Ottery home, or at least stopped by.

G
Bishop John de Grandisson
c.1292 – 1369 · Bishop of Exeter

The man who remade Ottery St Mary. As Bishop of Exeter, Grandisson rebuilt St Mary's Church in 1342, consciously modelling it on Exeter Cathedral with twin transept towers. He established the Collegiate Church, oversaw the Lady Chapel and chantry chapels, and is credited with the astronomical clock. His carved arms appear on five of the surviving misericords.

S
Samuel Taylor Coleridge
1772 – 1834 · Romantic Poet

Born on 21st October 1772 in the vicarage of St Mary's Church, Coleridge is Ottery's most celebrated son — tenth child of the Reverend John Coleridge, vicar and schoolmaster. He went on to co-author Lyrical Ballads with Wordsworth (1798), effectively founding English Romanticism, and wrote The Rime of the Ancient Mariner. A plaque inside the church marks his birth.

C
Cecily Bonville, Baroness Harington
c.1460 – 1529 · Noblewoman & Patron

As 7th Baroness Harington and wife of the Marquess of Dorset, Cecily commissioned the magnificent Dorset Aisle around 1520. Its elaborate fan vaulting remains one of the finest examples of Perpendicular Gothic architecture in the West Country — a lasting monument to her wealth and taste.

P
John Coleridge Patteson
1827 – 1871 · Missionary Bishop

Educated at King's School, Ottery, Patteson became a missionary to the South Sea Islands, founded schools across Melanesia, and learned an astonishing 23 local languages. He was martyred in 1871 and is now recognised as a saint in the Anglican Communion. A monument to him stands near Fenny Bridges in the parish.

W
William of Orange
1650 – 1702 · King William III of England

In 1688 the future King William III passed through Ottery St Mary on his march from Brixham to London during the Glorious Revolution. He stopped for dinner — a brief but remarkable moment placing this sleepy Devon town at the very centre of English constitutional history.

Chapter IV

Traditions & Folklore

Flaming barrels, captured bell-ringers, and mischievous pixies. Ottery takes its traditions very seriously — and very gleefully.

🔥 The Flaming Tar Barrels

Every 5th November, the streets of Ottery St Mary are taken over by one of the most extraordinary — and dangerous — traditions in England. Local people carry enormous burning barrels of tar through packed crowds on their bare shoulders. Only Otteregians (born-and-bred locals) are allowed to carry them. The tradition is believed to date to around the time of the Gunpowder Plot in 1605, and Ottery is the only town in England where it still takes place. Barrels can weigh up to 30 kg when alight, passed from carrier to carrier as they charge through the streets. Thousands travel from across the country to watch each year — from a safe distance.

🧚 Pixie Day

Each June, Ottery celebrates Pixie Day — a cheerful re-enactment of a beloved local legend. The story goes that when a bishop ordered church bells to be brought from Wales, local pixies cast a spell on the monks escorting them, leading them towards the cliffs at Sidmouth. One monk broke the spell by stubbing his toe and crying "God bless my soul!" The bells were saved — but the pixies exacted their revenge. Each year, children dress as pixies, capture the town's bell ringers, and imprison them in Pixies' Parlour until the Vicar arrives to rescue them.

Chapter V

Literary Connections

From Romantic poetry to the wizarding world — Ottery St Mary's outsized influence on English literature.

Few English towns of its size can match Ottery St Mary's literary credentials. Its church, its river, and its eccentric character have inspired Romantic poets, Victorian novelists — and one of the most beloved fantasy series ever written.

Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834)

The greatest of Ottery's literary sons grew up in the very vicarage of St Mary's Church, where his father served as both vicar and headmaster. The Devon landscape — its skies, its River Otter, its ancient church — fed the imagination that would produce some of English literature's most visionary and strange poetry.

With William Wordsworth, Coleridge published Lyrical Ballads in 1798 — widely regarded as the founding text of the English Romantic movement. His own poem The Rime of the Ancient Mariner remains one of the most reprinted poems in the English language.

"In Xanadu did Kubla Khan / A stately pleasure-dome decree..."
— Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Kubla Khan (1816), written after a dream during his Devon years
